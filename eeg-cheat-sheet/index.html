01

What EEG Actually Measures

EEG stands for electroencephalography. It records tiny voltage fluctuations detected at the scalp using electrodes placed on the head.

Those signals do not come from a single neuron. They reflect the summed activity of large populations of neurons, especially synchronized postsynaptic activity in the cortex.

EEG is not a mind-reading tool. It does not decode private thoughts in a direct way.

What it does well is show how brain activity changes over time, millisecond by millisecond.

That is EEG's strength: excellent temporal resolution. Compared with some brain imaging methods, its spatial precision is more limited, so it is better at telling you when activity changes than exactly where it started.

Key idea

EEG is best thought of as a fast, noninvasive way to track changes in brain activity over time.

02

Common Beginner Mistakes

These are the mistakes people make most often when they first start inspecting EEG recordings in VisualBCI.

1

Assuming every waveform is meaningful brain activity

Many visible changes come from eyes, face muscles, movement, or hardware issues.

2

Treating brain waves as rigid labels for mental states

Alpha, beta, and theta are useful categories, but real brains are more mixed and context-dependent.

3

Ignoring artifacts

Artifact recognition is not optional. It is part of basic EEG literacy.

4

Overinterpreting a single short segment

One interesting-looking moment rarely tells the full story without more context.

5

Confusing amplitude with “better” or “worse” activity

Bigger signals are not automatically more important, healthier, or more meaningful.

6

Forgetting that electrode location matters

Where the signal is measured changes what patterns are most plausible to expect.

03

The Five Main Brain Waves

These bands are frequency ranges commonly used to describe EEG rhythms in VisualBCI and other EEG tools.

They are useful shorthand, not rigid labels. A band can become more or less prominent depending on task, location, state, and artifact.

Delta

~0.5–4 Hz

Often associated with deep sleep

Very slow oscillations. In healthy awake adults, strong delta can be unusual outside specific contexts.

Theta

~4–8 Hz

Often linked to drowsiness, memory processes, meditation

Slightly faster than delta and common in transitional states. It can also appear during focused internal processing.

Alpha

~8–12 Hz

Relaxed wakefulness, especially with eyes closed

One of the easiest rhythms for beginners to recognize, especially over posterior regions.

Beta

~12–30 Hz

Alert thinking and active mental engagement

Faster, lower-amplitude activity often seen during wakeful, engaged states.

Gamma

~30+ Hz

Often discussed in relation to complex processing

Interesting in theory, but harder to measure cleanly with scalp EEG because muscle activity can contaminate the same range.

Brain waves are descriptive categories, not one-to-one translations of your mental state.
04

What Changes When You Close Your Eyes

In many people, alpha activity becomes more prominent when the eyes are closed and the person is relaxed but awake.

This effect is often strongest in posterior or occipital regions, where visual processing is heavily represented. When the eyes open, the rhythm often becomes less obvious.

In VisualBCI, this is easiest to spot by comparing the same posterior channels across short eyes-open and eyes-closed segments instead of comparing unrelated moments from different parts of a session.

Takeaway

Closing your eyes often increases alpha rhythm because the visual system is less engaged.

What to look for
  • A clearer repeating rhythm over posterior channels
  • A stronger alpha pattern without assuming every subject looks the same
  • Consistent comparisons: same channel, same posture, similar alertness

Signal vs Artifact

Not everything in an EEG trace you see in VisualBCI is brain activity.

One of the first real EEG skills in VisualBCI is learning to distinguish signal from artifact.

Eye blinks

Large, slow deflections that are often strongest in frontal channels.

Jaw clenching / muscle activity

Fast, noisy, high-frequency contamination that can make the trace look fuzzy.

Movement

Abrupt disturbances, sharp shifts, or wandering baseline changes from motion.

Poor electrode contact / line noise

Unstable channels or repetitive unwanted patterns caused by bad contact or electrical interference.

08

How EEG Analysis Works

The important shift for beginners is moving from “this trace looks interesting” to a repeatable process for checking quality, comparing conditions, and only then drawing conclusions.

Common workflow
Record Clean Inspect Analyze Interpret

Raw signal

EEG begins as voltage changing over time across multiple channels.

Filter and clean

Signals are often filtered and cleaned to remove obvious contamination before interpretation.

Segment

Data can be broken into shorter windows or event-related segments to isolate specific moments.

Analyze frequency

Methods such as spectral analysis estimate power in bands like alpha or beta.

Visualize

Plots, spectra, and topographies in VisualBCI make patterns easier to compare across channels and conditions.

Interpret

Context matters: task, subject state, channel location, and artifact all shape the reading.

Beginner habit that helps

When something looks meaningful, first ask whether it repeats, whether it changes across conditions, and whether artifact could explain it just as well.
